# Color Study for Logos

## Color Psychology

### Red
- **Emotions**: Energy, passion, urgency, excitement, power
- **Industries**: Food, entertainment, technology, retail
- **Examples**: Coca-Cola, YouTube, Netflix, Target
- **Use**: Calls to action, dynamic and youthful brands

### Blue
- **Emotions**: Trust, security, professionalism, calm, stability
- **Industries**: Technology, finance, healthcare, corporate
- **Examples**: Facebook, IBM, Samsung, PayPal, LinkedIn
- **Use**: Brands that need to convey trust and credibility

### Green
- **Emotions**: Nature, growth, health, sustainability, harmony
- **Industries**: Environment, healthcare, natural food, finance
- **Examples**: Spotify, Starbucks, WhatsApp, Animal Planet
- **Use**: Eco-friendly brands, wellness and growth

### Yellow
- **Emotions**: Optimism, joy, creativity, attention, warmth
- **Industries**: Food, entertainment, children, retail
- **Examples**: McDonald's, IKEA, Snapchat, National Geographic
- **Use**: Accessible and cheerful brands, attention-grabbing

### Orange
- **Emotions**: Enthusiasm, adventure, accessibility, youth
- **Industries**: Technology, food, sports, entertainment
- **Examples**: Amazon, Fanta, Firefox, SoundCloud
- **Use**: Innovative and accessible brands

### Purple/Violet
- **Emotions**: Luxury, creativity, wisdom, mystery, royalty
- **Industries**: Beauty, technology, luxury, education
- **Examples**: Twitch, Cadbury, Hallmark, Yahoo
- **Use**: Premium, creative, and sophisticated brands

### Pink
- **Emotions**: Femininity, softness, romance, youth, modernity
- **Industries**: Fashion, beauty, food, technology
- **Examples**: Barbie, T-Mobile, Lyft, Dribbble
- **Use**: Young, modern, and bold brands

### Black
- **Emotions**: Elegance, sophistication, power, luxury, modernity
- **Industries**: Fashion, luxury, technology, automotive
- **Examples**: Nike, Chanel, Adidas, Apple
- **Use**: Premium and minimalist brands

### White/Silver
- **Emotions**: Purity, simplicity, cleanliness, modernity
- **Industries**: Technology, healthcare, fashion, luxury
- **Examples**: Apple, Tesla, Wikipedia
- **Use**: Minimalism, negative space, contrast

## Color Harmonies

### Monochromatic
- Variations of a single color (hue + saturation + lightness)
- Elegant and cohesive, never conflicts
- Ideal for minimalist logos

### Complementary
- Two opposite colors on the color wheel
- High contrast and visual impact
- E.g., Blue + Orange, Red + Green

### Analogous
- 2-3 adjacent colors on the color wheel
- Natural and smooth harmony
- E.g., Blue + Cyan + Green

### Triadic
- Three equidistant colors on the color wheel
- Vibrant and balanced
- E.g., Red + Blue + Yellow

### Split-Complementary
- One base color + two colors adjacent to its complement
- Contrast with less tension than pure complementary

## Logo Color Rules

### Number of Colors
- **Ideal**: 1-2 main colors + 1 accent color
- **Recommended maximum**: 3-4 colors (e.g., Google, Microsoft)
- Each extra color adds reproduction complexity

### Contrast
- Minimum 4.5:1 contrast ratio for accessibility (WCAG AA)
- Test in grayscale to verify hierarchy
- Logo must work in pure black and white

### Color Versions
- **Full color version**: Complete brand colors
- **Dark background version**: Light colors, white/light text
- **Light background version**: Dark colors, black/dark text
- **Monochrome version**: Black or white only
- **Grayscale version**: For limited contexts

### AI Instruction
Choose colors that reflect the personality and industry of the brand described in the prompt. Use professional color harmony. Limit the palette to 2-3 main colors. For light background version: use more saturated and darker colors. For dark background version: use lighter and more vibrant colors. Maintain high contrast and mentally test in grayscale.
